#  Global Sensitivity Analysis of Prebiotic DNA Selection (v4.4)

##  Overview
This repository contains a comprehensive Global Sensitivity Analysis (GSA) using the Sobol Method to investigate the chemical selection of DNA nucleotides under prebiotic conditions. The simulation explores the stability and enrichment of Thymine (T) relative to other bases (U, C, A, G) in a high-temperature, alkaline environment.

###  Simulation Environment
- Temperature: $76.0^\circ\text{C}$
- pH: $9.5$
- Bases involved: 5-base system (U, T, C, A, G)
- Key Feature: Integration of Lipid Membranes and Clay Protection.

---

##  Technical Implementation

### 1. Sobol Method
Unlike local sensitivity analysis, we employed the Sobol Method to evaluate the variance of the output (Thymine Enrichment) based on 18 independent input parameters.

- Total Samples: $N = n \times (2k + 2)$ where $n=2000$ and $k=18$.
- Total Runs: $\approx 76,000$ simulations.
- Computational Strategy: Forced 4-worker parallel processing to maximize CPU throughput while maintaining system stability.

### 2. Parameter Space
The analysis considers 18 empirical parameters, including:
- Activation Energies ($E_a$): For hydrolysis and deamination of all 5 bases.
- Pre-exponential Factors ($A$): Kinetic constants for chemical decay.
- Environmental Factors: Lipid protection, Clay protection, and Base Catalysis factors.
- UV Resistance: Specific ratios for T, C, A, and G.

---

##  Key Findings & Analysis

###  The Dominant Drivers
The analysis reveals that Thymine enrichment is governed by a small subset of parameters, while the rest act as background noise.

1. Base Catalysis Factor ($S_T \approx 0.61$): The primary driver. The overall catalytic environment of the prebiotic soup determines the bulk of the selection pressure.
2. Uracil Decay Rate ($A_U$) ($S_T \approx 0.45$): The "Competitor Removal" effect. The enrichment of Thymine is more dependent on the rapid destruction of Uracil than on the inherent stability of Thymine itself.
3. Guanine (G) Neutrality: Despite the addition of Guanine to the system, its total-order index remains low, confirming that $G$ does not hinder the selective advantage of Thymine.

###  S1 vs ST Interpretation
- First-order Index ($S_1$): Represents the direct effect of a parameter.
- Total-order Index ($S_T$): Represents the direct effect plus all interactions with other parameters.
- Observation: The proximity of $S_1$ and $S_T$ for most variables indicates a robust and additive system. The high $S_T$ for `base_catalysis` and `A_U` suggests a synergistic interaction between the environment and the removal of competitors.

#  Final Conclusion: The Chemical Selection of Thymine in Prebiotic Environments

### 1. The "Competitor Removal" Hypothesis
The most significant discovery of this study is that the enrichment of Thymine (T) is not merely a result of its own inherent stability, but is primarily driven by the rapid and selective elimination of its primary competitor, Uracil (U). The Sobol analysis reveals that the pre-exponential factor for Uracil degradation ($A_U$) is a dominant driver ($S_T \approx 0.45$), confirming that the "cleansing" of the prebiotic pool from Uracil is a prerequisite for the emergence of DNA-like stability.

### 2. The Role of Environmental Catalysis
The system is heavily governed by the Base Catalysis Factor ($S_T \approx 0.61$). This indicates that the specific chemical environment (alkalinity and presence of catalysts) acts as the primary "filter." The high total-order index suggests that the environment does not act alone but interacts synergistically with the degradation rates of the nucleotides to accelerate the selection of Thymine.

### 3. Robustness Against Multi-Base Competition (The G-Factor)
By expanding the model to a 5-base system (U, T, C, A, G), this research proves that the addition of Guanine (G) does not disrupt the selective advantage of Thymine. Guanine remains chemically neutral in the context of Thymine enrichment, demonstrating that the "Thymine-selection mechanism" is robust even in more complex, realistic prebiotic mixtures.

### 4. Stability and Predictability
The close alignment between the First-order ($S_1$) and Total-order ($S_T$) indices suggests a highly stable and additive system. This means that the enrichment of Thymine is a predictable chemical outcome of the defined conditions ($T=76^\circ\text{C}, pH=9.5$) and is not sensitive to random fluctuations in secondary parameters (such as UV resistance or clay protection), which showed minimal influence.


###  Final Summary

The results obtained from the *UPDSF v4.4 framework provide a plausible chemical scenario for the selective enrichment of Thymine (T) in prebiotic environments. Rather than a single decisive factor, the data suggests that Thymine's emergence is likely the result of a combination of environmental and kinetic pressures.

The analysis indicates that the rapid degradation of Uracil (U), coupled with the general base catalysis of the environment, plays a more significant role in the enrichment process than the stability of Thymine alone. This suggests that the chemical "filtering" of competing bases may have been a key driver in the transition toward DNA-like genetic material.

Furthermore, the inclusion of Guanine (G) in the 5-base model shows that the presence of additional nucleotides does not appear to significantly hinder this selection process, suggesting that the mechanism remains viable even in more complex chemical mixtures.

While these simulations are based on a specific set of empirical parameters ($T=76^\circ\text{C}, pH=9.5$), the stability of the Sobol indices suggests that this outcome is consistent across a reasonable range of variability. In conclusion, this model offers a suggestive pathway for how the chemical laws of the early Earth could have favored the selection of Thymine, contributing to our understanding of the prebiotic origins of the genetic code.
